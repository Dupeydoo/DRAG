1. So, what is a Genetic Algorithm?

Glad you asked! A Genetic Algorithm, or GA for short, models biological evolution. It was originally invented by John Holland in the 1960s as a way to study evolution. You can find all of the original ideas in his book "Adaptation in Natural and Artificial systems" published in 1975. However, it was soon realised that the GA proved invaluable in solving complex optimisation problems. Nowadays the GA is being used in a huge variety of fields, including robotics, Artificial Intelligence in games, and even complex multi-objective research projects.

Some examples:

  • A knowledge based genetic algorithm for path planning of a mobile robot
  • Using a genetic algorithm to tune first-person shooter bots
  • A Fast Elitist Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm for Multi-objective Optimization: NSGA-II

2. How does it work?

In a GA, a population of solutions are maintained. Each solution is a different way of solving a chosen problem. These solutions have an encoding. For instance, a single solution may be represented by a binary string. Each bit would represent an on or off state in this case. There are many other ways of encoding, one can use real values, vectors and even matrices.

Each solution to a problem has a fitness assigned to it by a given fitness function. This fitness is a representation of how good a solution to the problem the candidate is. These fitness functions are usually automated and mathematical, however, in this project I employ a technique known as Interactive Evolutionary Computation (IEC). Since music is so subjective the usual fitness function is replaced by a human mentor who listens to and rates tracks. This means each user can get a unique output based on their taste in music.

Once the population has been evaluated, selection is performed using a selection algorithm. This selection of candidates decides which candidates will propagate to the next generation. This is done based on fitness value, with some allowance for less fitter candidates to be chosen to maintain diversity in the population. Selection is usually performed without replacement for a number of times equal to the population size.

Selected candidates are then subjected to genetic operators, namely crossover and mutation. Crossover simulates fusion of haploid genes in reproduction and mutation simulates random genetic changes to alleles or genes. Once these operations are performed then a replacement mechanism replaces the old population with the new population. This whole process is repeated until an optimum population of solutions are obtained.

In depth information can be found in this wonderful book by Mitchell Melanie. It features not only the mechanics of the GA, but also the history and a thorough explanation of all the concepts involved.

3. Why would I use a GA to generate music?

An excellent question! Even a simple phrase of music can take millions or even billions of possible arrangements. For instance, in the case of DRAG, there are eight beats which can each take sixteen different values. As order matters we can calculate that the number of ordered permutations possible is 16 to the 8th power, or 4,294,967,296, a huge number!

Thus, a GA which excels in solving complex optimisation problems with large search spaces is the ideal candidate. Also by using IEC, I can easily model the usually impossible function that a human brain creates when listening to their favourite music. More fascinating information about the human brain's response to music can be found in this paper.

4. How do I use the tool?

Start by clicking the Diversify Now option in the home menu. The process starts by providing an input track for the GA. You may do this from one of two forms. The first form has one drop-down for each beat in the bar, where you may pick an instrument/drum to use for that beat. The second form features a number of presets you may choose to save the time of building a track.

Once you have submitted one of the forms the GA generates the first population with some copies of your input track included. Then the fitness form is displayed. In this form you must listen to tracks and give them a rating between 0 and 10 in the provided inputs. Once you click submit the GA performs its operations and rebinds the new population for the next generation. This generation is once again displayed in the form for the next generation. This process continues until 10 generations have been rated, then a Machine Learning algorithm uses the fitness data you have provided previously to automate hundreds of generations instantly.
